The most important new reporting this weekend isn't about red lines — it's about the gap between them. Axios reports that OpenAI's Pentagon contract does not explicitly prohibit the collection of Americans' *publicly available* information. The contract language covers "unconstrained" collection of *private* information only.

**Why this matters:** Anthropic specifically asked the Pentagon to prohibit bulk collection of public data — geolocation, web browsing history, personal financial information purchased from data brokers. All of it legal to collect. All of it supercharged by AI at scale. The Pentagon refused. Anthropic held the line. OpenAI signed without that protection.

**The third red line reframed:** OpenAI's claim of "stronger guardrails" including a third prohibition on "high-stakes automated decisions" looks different in this light. They added a restriction the Pentagon was comfortable with while leaving out the one restriction the Pentagon explicitly rejected from Anthropic. The third red line isn't stronger — it's *different*, and strategically so.

**OpenAI retains:** full discretion over its safety stack, cloud-only deployment (no edge/autonomous weapons), cleared OpenAI personnel monitoring use, and "strong contractual protections." These are real. But contracts built on "all lawful purposes" depend on how the current administration interprets "lawful."

## Blood Moon Tuesday: Last Total Lunar Eclipse for Three Years

The total lunar eclipse Tuesday night/Wednesday morning (March 3) is the last one until New Year's Eve 2028. Totality lasts 58 minutes.

**Pacific Northwest timing:** The eclipse begins overnight and totality hits in the pre-dawn hours. Best viewing is from the western half of North America. Look west with an unobstructed horizon — the Moon will be low.

**The bonus:** A rare selenelion may be visible — an atmospheric refraction effect where you can briefly see both the rising sun and the eclipsed blood moon at the same time. Physics says this shouldn't happen (they're 180° apart). The atmosphere bends the light enough to make it possible.

This is worth getting up early for. ### The Gap That Matters

The most revealing detail this weekend isn't the third red line — it's the *missing* one. OpenAI's deal doesn't prohibit bulk collection of publicly available information about Americans. Anthropic demanded that prohibition. The Pentagon refused. Then the Pentagon accepted OpenAI without it.

This reframes the entire narrative. It was never "Anthropic wanted restrictions the Pentagon wouldn't accept." It was "Anthropic wanted a *specific* restriction — on public data collection — and the Pentagon would rather blacklist an American company than agree to it." That tells you more about what the Pentagon actually wants to do with AI than any press release about red lines.

The "stronger guardrails" framing is increasingly a distraction from the substantive question: will the Pentagon use AI to aggregate publicly available data about Americans at scale? Under current law, that's legal. Under Anthropic's proposed terms, it would have been prohibited. Under OpenAI's terms, it's not addressed. The absence speaks louder than the additions.
