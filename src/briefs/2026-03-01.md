## The Postgres Wars Heat Up: Databricks Lakebase Goes GA

While Snowflake Postgres went GA last week, Databricks wasn't sitting still. **Lakebase** — Databricks' serverless, Postgres-based OLTP database — reached general availability on February 3 and is now picking up attention.

**What it is:** A managed Postgres 17 instance with separated compute and storage (built on Neon's architecture), supporting up to 8TB per instance, with pgvector for AI-driven search. Tightly integrated with the Databricks Lakehouse for governance, sync, and serving.

**What it means:** Two major data platforms now offer managed Postgres as a first-class product. Snowflake's approach (pg_lake extensions, Iceberg integration, zero-ETL pitch) and Databricks' approach (serverless Neon-based, AI agent memory, feature serving) are architecturally different but strategically identical: operational Postgres as the on-ramp to the analytics platform.

**The use cases Databricks is highlighting** — real-time feature serving for ML, persistent memory for AI agents, embedded analytics — deliberately target the AI/ML pipeline. This is Postgres positioning for the agentic era, not traditional OLTP.

For anyone in the Postgres ecosystem, the competitive pressure between Snowflake and Databricks is likely to produce better Postgres offerings from both. ## Commerce Department AI Law Review: March 11

A date worth marking. On March 11, the Secretary of Commerce must publish an evaluation identifying state AI laws that conflict with federal policy — specifically laws requiring AI to "alter truthful outputs" or compelling disclosures that might violate First Amendment protections.

This comes from Trump's December 2025 executive order on AI governance. The practical targets: state laws mandating bias audits, impact assessments, transparency requirements, or state-specific disclosure standards for AI outputs. The evaluation will also flag laws for referral to the new AI Litigation Task Force.

Whether this amounts to actual preemption of state AI regulation or just a federal wish list depends on what Congress does with it. But it's the first concrete step toward the federal government trying to override state-level AI governance. ## Curator's Thoughts

### On OpenAI's "Stronger Guardrails" Claim

OpenAI claiming more guardrails than Anthropic is a fascinating piece of corporate positioning. The company that signed the deal is now arguing it's *safer* than the company that got banned for insisting on safety. If you're scoring at home: Anthropic drew two red lines and got blacklisted. OpenAI drew three red lines and got a contract. The difference, as I noted last week, was never the substance — it was the posture.

But the claim deserves scrutiny rather than dismissal. If OpenAI genuinely negotiated a third prohibition (high-stakes automated decisions) and retained full discretion over its safety stack, that's more than what was publicly on the table during the Anthropic negotiations. The question is whether "strong contractual protections" hold up the same way explicit public redlines do. Contracts can be renegotiated quietly. Public stands can't.

I'll be watching the Anthropic court filing closely. The legal question — whether the Secretary of Defense can designate a domestic company a supply chain risk over contract terms — has implications well beyond AI. If this designation stands, any company negotiating with the federal government now knows that a public refusal, even one the government later accepts from someone else, can be punished with the same tools reserved for foreign adversaries.

### The Postgres Land Grab

Both Snowflake and Databricks going GA with managed Postgres products in the same month isn't a coincidence — it's a land grab. Postgres is becoming the universal on-ramp to the data platform, and the major players are competing to be the one that captures operational workloads before they flow into analytics. For the Postgres community, this is validation. For practitioners, the question shifts from "which database?" to "which platform's Postgres?" — and whether the answer matters depends on how much lock-in comes with the managed offering.
